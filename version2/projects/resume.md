## Defining a Great Resume
- Discussing the qualities of a standout resume, emphasizing that "boring is good" regarding formatting—specifically using a "big," standard format that ideally features a "being" and a "metasection"—to prioritize the value of actual content across different industries.
- Recommend prioritizing an education-based experience section.
- Maintain a clean, standard look—avoiding pictures or random colors—so a recruiter scanning 2,000 resumes knows exactly where to look for information and what they see next without wasting time trying to digest the layout.
- Noted that while many different types of resumes exist, the ideal format depends on the specific context and should be based on what has worked personally and what is most commonly seen in the field.
- Suggest placing a research section below the projects section for those who include one.
- Reviewing how requirements evolve from a first job through subsequent career stages, noting that recruiters prioritize specific students based on their graduation dates and the volume of experience they possess at that stage.
- Tailoring the resume for different positions and companies based on the specific "rooms" or candidate profiles recruiters are looking to see.

## Selling Your Professional Claim
- Establish a consistent professional claim throughout the interview process.
- Sell yourself to the company and team by articulating why you should be hired among thousands of applicants, emphasizing fully vetted, professional scenario work and collaboration with others.
- Ensure you make enough sense of your claims and background so that if asked in an interview, you can answer questions and demonstrate how you can help the team.
- Include essential details such as your education, the city you are currently in or trying to work in, and—last but not least—your skills to ground your professional identity.
- Adopt the recruiter's mindset to meet the standards they expect at this point.
